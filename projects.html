Projects

Project 1: Clouded Skies--Creative Coding

In my junior year spring semester, I participated in CISC 259, or Creative Coding, which was both an entertaining and enlightening experience. My final project of the class was to make an art piece utilizing some or all of the coding techniques I had learned throughout the semester; while others made interactive games, digital planners, and visual spectacles, I opted to make a more meditative sensory experience. Using minimalistic art design and Perlin noise, I simulated the flocking of birds as they soared through the air! I also included parallax-scrolling hills with trees, a day/night cycle, and an occasional train that would whizz by the screen. Finally, I wrote and synthesized a calming musical number to play in the background and tie all the elements together. Not too shabby, if I do say so myself!

Project 2: Veterinarian's Internal Database--Database Design

For my Database Design (CISC 450) final project last semester, I worked with a couple of my classmates (Georgia and Alejandro) to make an interactable database for a mock veterinarian company. This database functioned as an internal HR database, keeping track of both employees and customers. We were able to provide services such as managing appointments, recording pet and owner information, storing employee data, and even individual pet visits and billing. Although we didn't put a lot of shine into the front-end of it, we put a lot of work into the design of the database itself, and I feel proud to have been part of this final project.

Project 3: After Hours--Game Production

As the final project of my Game Production class, I worked with a cohort and classmate Eli Goldberg to create a thrilling pixel-art, top-down horror experience. Set in a barren, maze-like school, After Hours has the protagonist desparately trying to escape a physical manifestation of his trauma; Mot only does this monster get more and more aggressive --he messes with the protagonist's sense of direction and self, inverting the player's controls to add an extra element of confusion. The protagonist does have some defensive capabilites though, as they can place down temporary walls that block and can even possibly trap the monster for a brief period of time. Eli did all of the heavy coding, including some inventive pathfinding methods and controller recombinations, and I focused on making the visual and sound assets for the game. This is by far one of the best examples of in-class projects that I've made and one of my greatest accomplishments from my time at the University of St. Thomas!
